Common questions about data center development in El Dorado

Land Use & Zoning

No zoning changes have been approved or granted.

  • No zoning changes have been approved or granted.
  • Any proposal must either comply with existing zoning or go through a public rezoning or Special Use Permit process.
  • Under Kansas law (K.S.A. 12-741 et seq.), zoning decisions are locally controlled and require:
    • Planning Commission review
    • Public notice and hearings
    • Final approval by the City Commission
  • The City of El Dorado is currently defining its standards and process before any project is considered, ensuring rules are in place first—not after the fact.

No. There are no active rezonings for data centers.

  • No. There are no active rezonings for data centers.
  • Rezoning cannot occur without:
    • A landowner application
    • Public notification
    • Public hearings
  • Compatibility with surrounding land uses is a legal requirement, not a preference.

    • Use of existing industrial land (including brownfield sites not suitable for housing)
    • Leveraging infrastructure assets (rail, highway, water access)
    • Expanding the commercial/industrial tax base to reduce pressure on residential taxpayers

Water Use & Supply

It depends on design. Newer facilities increasingly use:

  • Air-cooled systems
  • Closed-loop systems that significantly reduce water demand

Under Kansas law (including SB 98 framework and state review processes), projects must submit:

  • Water use plans
  • Conservation strategies
  • Operational disclosures

Primary source: El Dorado Reservoir, for which the City holds water rights.

Options include:

  • Treated water
  • Raw water via existing infrastructure
  • Reclaimed wastewater

Current data shows:

  • The lake has remained full or near full ~89% of the time since 1995

The City has:

  • ~10 million gallons/day already allocated for industrial growth
  • Additional long-term capacity identified through studies (including Black & Veatch analysis)

Importantly:

  • Much of this water currently passes downstream unused under federal reservoir management
  • Residential supply is legally and operationally prioritized first.

Environmental Impact

Yes. Projects must comply with:

  • State environmental permitting
  • Federal regulations (e.g., Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act if applicable)

Kansas has added heightened scrutiny for large-scale data infrastructure through recent legislation and review processes.

Data centers are low-emission facilities compared to traditional industry.

  • Backup generators:
    • Are rarely used
    • Must meet strict air quality standards
  • Noise:
    • Typically comparable to or less than highway traffic
    • Managed through setbacks, berms, and sound attenuation design

Economic Impact

Fewer—but higher-quality jobs:

  • Skilled, technical positions
  • Strong wages and benefits

Most importantly:

  • Significant construction employment (often hundreds of jobs over multiple years)
  • Workforce partnerships with community colleges and technical schools

No local incentives have been approved or offered.

  • No local incentives have been approved or offered.
  • Any local incentives (e.g., IRBs under K.S.A. 12-1740 et seq.):
    • Must be publicly reviewed and approved
  • At the state level:
    • Kansas provides sales tax exemptions on data center equipment (SB 98 framework), similar to other large capital investments
  • These are performance-based and time-limited, not permanent subsidies.

Revenue streams include:

  • Property taxes (especially after abatements expire)
  • Utility franchise fees
  • Water sales (new municipal revenue source)

Data centers represent 30+ year capital investments, not short-term operations.
