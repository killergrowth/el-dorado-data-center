Property Taxes & Fiscal Impact

The Short Version

  • •The Question: Will this raise my property taxes?
  • •What We Found: The City of El Dorado can abate up to 85% of property taxes for up to ten years either through the issuance of Industrial Revenue Bonds (IRBs) or through an Economic Development Exemption (EDX). This a common tool to attract major investments, but also facilitates at least 15% of the assessed value to be collected during the abatement period.
  • •After abatements end, the facility pays full property taxes. A billion-dollar-plus facility adds a lot of taxable value to the community that could be used for our roads, schools and other improvements.
  • •When the tax base grows, tax rates can go down for everyone—if local budgets stay similar. That means your tax bill could decrease over time.
  • •Important: This isn't guaranteed. It depends on what local officials decide to do with budgets in the future.

Power, Water & Infrastructure

The Short Version

The Question: Will this strain our electric grid or raise my electric bill?

What We Found: In some parts of the country, data centers have raised electric bills when costs were shared across all customers. However, Kansas put protections in place in November 2025 requiring large power users to pay for their own infrastructure under separate rate structures from residential customers. The federal government has also proposed policies requiring data centers to pay more for new generation than homeowners. These safeguards are designed to prevent your electric bill from going up due to data center power demands.

The Question: Will this use up our water?

What We Found: Older designs use a lot of water, but modern facilities can use air cooling or water-saving designs that recycle water. El Dorado is in a unique position however: it has approximately 10 million gallons per day for industrial use. This usage fits within the city's long-term water planning.

The Question: What about roads, schools, and city services?

What We Found: Data centers employ far fewer people than other industries with similar investments. That means less traffic, less demand for housing, and less pressure on schools compared to a factory or distribution center. That also means once tax abatements end, there can potentially be more money for roads and schools.

Community Concerns & Safety

The Short Version

The Concern: Noise from cooling systems and generators

What We Found: Modern facilities use sound barriers and equipment enclosures to significantly reduce noise transmission.

The Concern: Backup generators and emissions

What We Found: Generators are tested monthly for short periods (usually 30 minutes). They meet federal emissions standards. Fuel is stored in safe, regulated tanks. Testing schedules can be required by the community.

The Concern: Fire risk from batteries and equipment

What We Found: Modern battery energy storage systems (BESS) include engineered safety features — such as battery management systems, thermal monitoring, spacing and separation requirements, and integrated fire detection and suppression provisions — and are governed by industry safety standards such as NFPA 855 and UL 9540A. A data center would work directly with our local fire department on emergency plans and provide specialized training and equipment.

The Concern: Visual impact and property values

What We Found: Large buildings can be screened with landscaping, setbacks, and design standards. Research on property value impact is mixed and depends heavily on how well the facility is integrated into the community.
